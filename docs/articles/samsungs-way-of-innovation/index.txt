<p>The July 3, 2006 issue of <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/" title="Business Week">Business Week</a> has a story on <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/magazine/content/06_27/b3991052.htm" title="Camp Samsung">how innovation happens at Samsung</a>. Samsung created a Value Innovation Program and have identified <a href="http://www.businessweek.com/magazine/content/06_27/b3991054.htm" title="Samsung&#x27;s Key Brainstorming Practices">key brainstorming practices</a>.</p>
<p>The following quotes should give you an idea how they go about solving problems. One key tenet: each project works against a <strong>fixed deadline</strong>. Another one: they bring together everyone at the beginning to thrash out differences.</p>
<blockquote class="markdown-quote">
  <p>Find that perfect balance of cost, innovation, and technology that makes a product great.</p>
  <p>Every step of the way, team members drew what Samsung calls "value curves." These are graphs that rank various attributes such as picture quality and design on a scale of 1 to 5, from outright bad to excellent.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The story also suggests that it is not enough to look at a competitor's product and replicate it. In today's competitive world a company has to redo its culture to stay in a leadership position and be able to release one great product after another.</p>

<p>When agility comes up in conversations, the typical topics are methods, techniques, or process, but rarely <em>initiative</em>. Even though initiative, more than other factors, determines if a team can perform at maximum performance. These days an organization cannot be competitive if its people are at levels 1 or 2 on the initiative scale.</p>
<h2>Levels of Initiative <sup><a href="#user-content-fn-1" id="user-content-fnref-1" data-footnote-ref aria-describedby="footnote-label">1</a></sup></h2>
<ol>
  <li><em>Wait</em> until told. The lowest level of initiative.</li>
  <li><em>Ask</em> what to do.</li>
  <li><em>Recommend</em>, and then take resulting action.</li>
  <li><em>Act</em>, but advise at once.</li>
  <li><em>Act on own</em>, then routinely report. The highest level of initiative.</li>
</ol>
<p>Managers often unwittingly kill people's initiatives, because managers make too many decisions that would better be done by others. If you notice that your manager is making a decision that could be handled more effectively by you, then recommend it. You might be surprised that he or she will gladly accept your assistance.</p>
<p>Do you think that your team could cut cycle time and improve quality if the team members acted at higher levels of initiative?</p>
